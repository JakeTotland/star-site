Project Teams

And Proposing New Projects

Last Updated Feb. 16, 2019


The project team is the fundamental unit of Star membership. Project teams are conceived by and consist of dedicated Star members interested in a single topic. Many have faculty mentors or corporate sponsors to assist them, but this is by no means a requirement. Project teams must be open to all members and are subject to a renewal vote at the beginning of each year.


All our project teams, past and present, are described on this page. Click the () tab on the left side of the screen to choose one from the list.


Any member of Star can start a new project team. To do so, one must submit a formatted proposal to the president or vice president. This officer will review the proposal—without making any judgments—to ensure it adheres to Star's constitution. Once the proposal is approved, the member proposing the project will present their ideas at the next possible general meeting. After the proposal presentation, and a quick question/answer period, Star members present at that meeting will vote on the project. If approved, the project team will immediately be created, the proposing member will be installed as team lead, and requested money/resources will be allocated for the team. Members will then have the opportunity to formally join.

Aerospike Test Stand

Led by

  • Vivek Vidyarthi

Last Updated Nov. 1, 2018


Conventional rocket nozzles are designed for a relatively small altitude range and as a result, require current rockets to have multiple stages, thereby increasing cost and complexity. Aerospike nozzles are potential replacements to conventional bell-shaped nozzle and allow for Single Stage to Orbit (SSTO) vehicles. The annular nozzle, through its altitude compensating characteristics, allows for efficient thrust on a wide altitude range and reduce complexity. In order to make SSTO feasible, altitude compensating nozzles must be further developed.


In previous research, the thermal and strength issues resulted in the reduction development, but current developments in additive manufacturing allow for possible refinement in manufacturing and thermal management. The aerospike team’s primary objective is to design, optimize, and outline the benefits of the aerospike.Along with the aerospike, we will be designing a static test bench with a functioning liquid motor that will be accessible to Star members. We are currently in the process of generating designs for an aerospike nozzle as well as outlining a basic liquid motor.

BEAM "Cube Satellite"

Led by

  • Thomas McGovern

Last Updated Dec. 13, 2018


3D printing, although still in its infancy as an engineering field, is one of the fastest growing. Additive manufacturing is appealing because it offers the ability to create parts with speed, convenience, and versatility unmatched by conventional manufacturing methods. It takes mere hours to go from an idea to a CAD model to a 3D printed part, in your hands. This convenience would obviously be extremely useful in remote environments, cut off from supplies, and what environment is more remote than space? NASA and the Made in Space program have already done extensive work with 3D printing thermoplastics in space, but no research has been done on 3D printing with thermosets in space. Thermoset 3D printing on earth by itself is a rather new field.


Inspired by the work done here at Rutgers University by the Mazzeo Research group on thermoset 3D printing, we are determined to carry out the first thermoset 3D printing experiment in space. To accomplish this, we are currently designing BEAM (Beyond Earth Additive Manufacturing), a microsatellite meant to carry out a contact-angle goniometry experiment on thermosetting resins in zero gravity. BEAM will extrude a droplet onto a heated bed, and take multiple photographs to monitor the droplet’s morphology as it cures over time. We hope to see decreased droplet spreading in the absence of gravity-driven spreading, opening the door to higher-resolutions in 3D printed thermoset parts.

School Outreach

Led by

  • Jennifer Mead
  • Lara Hirsch

Last Updated Oct. 18, 2018


The School Outreach team is focused on bringing space-related STEM outreach programs to elementary, middle and high schools in New Jersey. Our intent is to demonstrate to students how the science that they learn can be applied to exploring and studying our universe, and to inspire them to be scientists and ask questions about the things they see. The Outreach team has already run a successful program at Roosevelt Elementary School in New Brunswick on the wonders and uses of light in studying and understanding space. We hope to continue to conduct fun and impactful programs with more schools in the future.


Our programs can be tailored to a particular classroom’s needs and interests, as many of the topics that are covered can also be applied in space, whether it be the weather or geology on other planets, how we use mechanics to understand orbits, or how we use our understanding of gravity to escape the Earth, or to set our GPS. Additionally, we hope to use our resources to provide additional space-related science resources to local schools to continue to inspire students to be excited about space and all that we have yet to learn.

Weather Balloon

Led by

  • Matthew Lesiw
Formerly
  • Orion Farr

Last Updated May 2, 2018


Each year, this team builds and launches a raspberry pi based weather monitoring experiment suite 30,000 meters into the sky using a weather balloon. The experiments carried onboard will record data that we will use to characterize the temperature, pressure, humidity, altitude, and light conditions found in the troposphere and stratosphere layers of the atmosphere. We hope launch a weather balloon as a yearly project in order to analyze trends in the data collected. The 8 ft diameter weather balloon requires 50 cubic feet of helium to carry a 1 lb payload to an approximate height of 72,000 ft. Reaching this altitude puts us well within the stratosphere allowing for measurements to be recorded that are not possible from the ground. This project also has the capacity to monitor climate change over time in New Jersey.

Board Game

Led by

  • Anthony Parfilko

Archived May 4, 2018


In the modern world, team-building experiences are vital to future success. It is difficult for individuals to single-handedly accomplish monumental tasks without help from a team. Knowing this, Star is producing a space-themed board game that teaches players the necessity of working as a team to foster success. Within our concepts are realistic elements of space exploration, including restrictions on viable space-faring destinations based on ship and cargo specs. We are currently in the Alpha stage of development and intend to begin production within a year.

Roadrunner

Led by

  • Matthew Lesiw

Archived May 4, 2018


The goal of this project team is to produce the first microrover derived from a well-established, well-documented, and well-tested spacecraft standard. That sounds like a lot, but all it means is that we’re looking to make a CubeSat into a small rover. For science!


We’re currently working on building a prototype—affectionately called Roadrunner—of what will eventually become the microrover standard. A bit like how biologists have “type specimens” to define a species, we’re going to lay out and define most of the physical and mechanical elements that will go into a very exhaustive document that will allow anyone to build our tiny rovers.

As of now, we’re in the middle of producing the skeleton on which the rest of the first prototype will be built. Eventually, it will be outfitted with the hardware needed to make what amounts to an RC car, and we’ll be taking it out into different environments to see how it handles—and adjust our design accordingly.

Speaker Series

Led by

  • Nada Ibrahim
  • Jessica Olson

Archived May 4, 2018


The Speaker Series is a monthly project in which Star invites professors, researchers, engineers, and anyone else working in the field of space or space exploration to Rutgers University to describe their career or work. We host events in the hope to spread knowledge about the field of aerospace and astrophysics. Our main goal is to educate space enthusiasts on different aspects within the field. By bringing outside speakers, connections are made for students to pursue careers with companies and organization such as SpaceX and NASA. We work hard to correspond with the best contacts we can find, and our main method of interaction with our events' audience tends to be through social media.
